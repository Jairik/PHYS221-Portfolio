Math is cool

Coming from a computer science and data science background, I have had immense exposure to seeing how numbers shape our decision making, serve as a tool for explaining things, and how numbers essentially power everything that we do. Despite knowing the calculus-derived formulas, linear algebra, and computational theory, I had a fundamentally weak understanding of how numbers actually make the world work. This class gave me tremendous insight into this, showcasing how the complexities such as motion and collisions have been studied and broken down into numerical expressions and conceptual interpretations, highlighting hundreds of years of study into how ultimately everything works. I am beyond grateful to have been able to take this class in my last semester of college, providing me an opportunity to finally connect everything I have learned through a low-level physics standpoint.

What ultimately stood out to me the most from this class was the way that it forced me to slow down and reflect. In computer science, there is always something to run, test, debug, or iterate on. Many of the more difficult problems in this course showcase this transition from 100 miles an hour (or 44.704 m/s) to decelerating slowly to near-rest, having to really think about the system and breaking down each component. Many of the problems selected for this portfolio reflect this shift, representing the times that I stopped looking at the math as simply a means to solve a problem, but instead a modality to describe and express what is actually happening in a scenario. If there is anything that I have learned through my education here, especially in data science classes, it is that math and numbers are beautiful in the sense that they can universally tell any sort of story. And what is a more beautiful application of this than the world around us?

— jjm · last semester, last class
